World Life Expectancy and Health Factors

Explore global life expectancies and health factors from around the world.

Life Expectancy refers to the average number of years a person can expect to live. It is a crucial indicator of overall health and well-being in a particular country or region. By analyzing life expectancy worldwide, we can gain valuable insights into the state of global public health.

This webpage provides interactive visualizations, charts, and maps that highlight key health indicators. Dive into the data to gain insights into the health and well-being of different countries throughout the 21st Century.

The data used for these visualizations is authentic and sourced from the World Health Organization (WHO). The information is accurate for the years 2000-2015 and is available for public use.

The Choropleth Map shown on the left, visualizes the Life Expectancies of the world countries, using a diverging scale.

The darker shades of Blue represents countries with High Life Expectancies. While the lighter shades of Blue/Green represents countries with Low Life Expectancies. The middle range is represented by a shade of Turquoise / Teal

The general results show that 'western countries' such as Australia, USA, UK, as well as parts of South America have Life Expectancies on the upper end of the scale. While most Central and South-East asia have life expectancies in the middle-range. And most parts of Central and South Africa have life expectancies at the lower end of the scale.

The Bubble Chart on the right, visualizes the Life Expectancies against the Gross Domestic Product of world countries. The countries are split into two (2) groups visually: Developing and Developed nations based on colour. Users can also select a year from 2000 - 2015 in which the chart displayed will represent for that year

In general, we can gather that Developed Nations tend to be placed around the top right area, which demonstrates higher Life Expectancies along with a higher GDP. While Developing Nations are all spread out in different areas. This may suggest that Developed Nations could have a positive correlation with having a high life expectancy and a high GDP

The Ranged Dot Plot above visualizes the change in the Life Expectancy of World Countries from 2000 - 2015. The light maroon and dark maroon points represent data in the Years 2000 and 2015 respectively. This chart effectively allows users to identify the magnitude in change of the Life Expectancy within 15 Years

Key Observations:
Some countries that show the highest increase in Life Expectancy (> +15) are: Zimbabwe, Eritrea, Zambia, Botswana, Rwanda, Uganda, Malawi.
Countries that showed a decrease (-) in Life Expectancy after 15 Years include: Estonia, Grenada, Iraq, Libya, Paraguay, Romania, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, Syrian Arab Republic, Yemen

The Trellis Scatter Plot above visualizes the Life Expectancy of World Countries against Immunization Coverage of Major Diseases (Hepatitis B, Polio, Diphtheria). The chart shows the Immunization percentage (%) coverage among One (1) Year Olds. It gives users a clear visual comparison of the data between these diseases.

The general results show that developed nations tend to be placed in the right upper corner of the chart, indicating high immunization coverage and high life expectancies. All three charts show the same pattern / structure and it can be argued that there is a strong correlation between high immunization coverage and high life expectancies.

The Trellis Scatter Plot above visualizes the Life Expectancy of World Countries against Measles Cases, HIV Deaths and Adult Mortality. The chart scales based on Per 1000 Population.

The results show that there are only 2 countries with extremely high reported Measles cases (>40,000): India and China. There are also 5 countires with high reported Measles cases (>10,000): Mongolia, Kyrgzstan, Ethiopia, Indonesia, Nigeria. High HIV/AIDS deaths (>6) do not seem to be very prominent in most countries except: Lesotho, Swaziland, Zimbabwe. The results also demonstrate that there is a high negative correlation between high life expectancy and high adult mortality. Most developed countries also have much lower Adult Mortality compared to Developing Nations.

The Bar Chart on the left shows the Average Schooling Years of Developed Nations. The results show that Developed Nations have quite similar Average Schooling years, which is an important factor in Life Expectancy.

The country with the highest average schooling years is Australia (20.4) and the country with the lowest average schooling years is Luxembourg (13.9) in 2015.

The Bar Chart on the right shows the Government Expenditure on Health as a percentage of the Total Government Expenditure in Developed Nations. The results show more variety in Governemnt Expenditure on Health for Developed Nations.

The country with the highest Government Expenditure on Health is United States of America (17.14%) and the country with the lowest Government Expenditure on Health is Japan (1.23%) in 2014. This could suggest things such as the priority of a Country's government on Health, or rather the severity / need of Expenditure on Health of a Country

The Scatterplot on the left visualizes the Average Body Mass Index of World Countries. The Chart separates the colors of the data point values into appropriate BMI ranges to represent the BMI indicators.

BMI Ranges:
18.5 and Under - Underweight
18.5 - 24.9 - Normal
25 - 29.9 - Overweight
30 - 34.9 - Obese
35 and Above - Extremely Obese

The results show a varied response in the BMIs of different countries. The top 3 countries with the highest averge BMIs are: Kiribati, Tonga, Samoa. The top 3 countries with the lowest average BMIs are: Madagascar, Mauritania, Sao Tome and Principe
